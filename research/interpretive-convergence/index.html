Published article series

Interpretive Convergence, Legal Acts, and Legal Agents

This research develops an account of legal meaning and legal normativity as emerging through interpretive convergence among legal actors. Legal meaning is not fixed by text alone, nor reducible to private interpretive intention. It is stabilized through role-bound legal acts, public reason-giving, uptake, contestation, and institutional correction.

Core Questions

  • How can a dispersed legal order function as a common normative practice?
  • How do legal acts acquire shared public meaning rather than remaining isolated utterances or documents?
  • What role do legal agents play in producing, receiving, contesting, and stabilizing legal meaning?
  • How should judicial authority be understood if it is mediated through uptake and response rather than self-authenticating finality?

Key Claims

  • Legal objectivity is practice-mediated: it is formed and sustained through public, role-bound legal activity.
  • Legal acts must satisfy conditions of generality, shareability, and focal-point stability to function as public norms.
  • Legal agents are not passive interpreters but participants in the formation, uptake, correction, and continuation of legal meaning.
  • Judicial authority is earned and mediated through public reason-giving, institutional role, review, and the legal community's structured uptake.
