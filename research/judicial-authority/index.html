Judicial reform and legal theory

Courts, Judicial Authority, and Institutional Legitimacy

This project reframes debates about courts and judicial reform around the institutional conditions of judicial authority. The central question is not simply how power should be distributed among courts, constitutional review bodies, and judicial administration, but how legal decisions become legitimate, final, reviewable, and publicly answerable.

Status

This is an active research and public-discussion project. It also supports conference-facing materials on Korean judicial reform, including speaker guidance, discussion agendas, and explanatory public copy.

The public purpose of this page is to state the conceptual frame. It does not present a final institutional design proposal.

Core Questions

  • Where does the authority of courts come from?
  • Is judicial reform primarily a matter of allocating power, or of reconstructing the conditions of legitimacy?
  • How should finality, error correction, constitutional review, and institutional role be balanced?
  • How should the relationship between the Supreme Court and the Constitutional Court be understood beyond institutional rivalry or hierarchy?
  • What does the debate over constitutional complaints against court judgments reveal about the structure of judicial authority?

Key Orientation

  • Judicial authority is not mere final decision-making power.
  • Legitimacy depends on public reason-giving, institutional role, review structure, and the capacity to resolve disputes while remaining accountable to law.
  • Judicial reform should not be reduced to administrative design, caseload management, or political advantage.
  • The most important theoretical task is to identify the assumptions about authority, finality, and legitimacy that different reform proposals presuppose.

Public Version Note
