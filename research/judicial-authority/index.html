Judicial reform and legal theory

Courts, Judicial Authority, and Institutional Legitimacy

This project examines judicial authority and institutional legitimacy in connection with contemporary debates about courts and judicial reform. It asks how legal decisions become authoritative, final, reviewable, and publicly answerable.

Status

This is an active research and public-discussion project. It also informs conference-facing materials on Korean judicial reform.

This page states the general research orientation. It does not present a final institutional design proposal.

Core Questions

  • Where does the authority of courts come from?
  • How should judicial reform be connected to the conditions of institutional legitimacy?
  • How should finality, error correction, constitutional review, and institutional role be balanced?
  • How should the relationship between ordinary courts and constitutional review be understood?

Orientation

  • Judicial authority is not mere final decision-making power.
  • Legitimacy depends on public reason-giving, institutional role, review structure, and the capacity to resolve disputes while remaining accountable to law.
  • The project asks what different reform proposals presuppose about authority, finality, and legitimacy.

Public Version Note

This page is a brief public orientation to a developing research and conference-facing project. It should not be read as a reform bill, institutional blueprint, or official position statement.
