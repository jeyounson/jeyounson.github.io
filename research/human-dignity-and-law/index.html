Book project

Human Dignity and Law

This book project reconstructs human dignity not as a mere constitutional slogan or all-purpose moral value, but as a legal status concept. It asks how dignity structures legal personality, rights, duties, responsibility, answerability, and the limits of state power.

Status

This is an ongoing book-scale manuscript project. The full manuscript is not posted here. This page is a public explanatory surface that gives the project's conceptual orientation and reading context.

The current working manuscript is being maintained in a private source-preserving authoring workflow before any public manuscript release.

Core Questions

  • Why does law speak in the language of human dignity?
  • Is dignity the ground of rights, the condition of rights, or a legal status that organizes rights and duties?
  • How does dignity relate to legal personhood, responsibility, and the demand for reasons?
  • What does dignity require for noncitizens, foreigners, criminal defendants, vulnerable persons, and those subject to state power?
  • How can dignity be legally robust without becoming an empty formula that decides every case in advance?

Key Orientation

  • Human dignity is best understood as a structural legal status rather than merely a high-ranking value.
  • Dignity requires that persons be addressed as rights-and-duties-bearing subjects, not merely as administrative objects or policy instruments.
  • Dignity links moral status to legal standing, procedural answerability, and robust legality.
  • Dignity does not automatically generate full citizenship rights or every concrete legal entitlement, but it constrains how legal orders may treat any person subject to law.
