Book project

Human Dignity and Law

This ongoing book project examines human dignity as a legal concept. Its central concern is how dignity relates to legal status, rights, duties, responsibility, answerability, and the limits of state power.

The book project is part of a broader research program connecting human dignity, legal expressivism, expressive harm, and interpretive convergence in law.

Connected program

Status

This is an ongoing manuscript project. The full manuscript is not posted here. This page provides only a short public description of the project.

Any future public excerpt or manuscript version will be identified separately and will carry its own version note.

Core Questions

  • Why does law speak in the language of human dignity?
  • How does dignity relate to legal status and legal personhood?
  • How do legal practices express, deny, preserve, or repair a person's standing?
  • What is the relation between dignity, rights, duties, and responsibility?
  • How should dignity constrain the exercise of public power?

Orientation

  • The project treats dignity as a legal concept with institutional and doctrinal consequences.
  • It pays particular attention to status, standing, answerability, and public justification.
  • It treats expressive harm as a diagnostic layer for identifying public status meanings in legal practice.
  • It uses interpretive convergence as a structural layer for asking how those meanings are stabilized, contested, corrected, or allowed to block standing.
  • It also asks how dignity can remain legally meaningful without becoming an empty formula.
