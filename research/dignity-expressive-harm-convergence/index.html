Connected research program

Human Dignity, Expressive Harm, and Interpretive Convergence

This research program connects my work on human dignity, legal expressivism, expressive harm, and the interpretive convergence theory of law. Across these projects, I ask how legal practices express, deny, preserve, or repair a person's standing as someone capable of making claims, receiving reasons, and contesting public power.

Program Structure

Normative problem

Human Dignity

The dignity work examines hard cases in which ordinary welfare, autonomy, or rights-based descriptions do not fully explain what has gone wrong. The central concern is legal standing: how a person appears before law as a bearer of claims, reasons, and answerability.

Diagnostic layer

Expressive Harm

The expressivist work asks what legal statements, decisions, and practices publicly mean. Expressive harm is not merely a private loss of self-meaning; it concerns the public status meaning that law performs, stabilizes, resists, or repairs.

Structural layer

Interpretive Convergence

The convergence work explains how legal meaning becomes stable through legal acts, institutional roles, public reason-giving, uptake, contestation, and correction. It supplies the structural account of how expressive meanings become part of legal practice.

Shared question

Standing and Public Justification

The shared question is whether legal practice preserves persons as possible claimants and addressees of reasons. This connects dignity's status dimension, expressive harm's public-meaning dimension, and convergence theory's practice-centered account of legal normativity.

Orientation

The program does not treat every offensive message or every disagreement as a dignity violation. Its focus is narrower: when a legal practice places a person outside the space of claim, response, uptake, and contestation, the wrong is not exhausted by material loss or subjective distress. At the same time, the account does not make human dignity a simple condition for the existence of law as such. The question is how far a legal order remains robustly answerable to the persons it governs.

In this sense, legal expressivism is the diagnostic layer and interpretive convergence is the structural layer. Expressivism identifies the public status meaning of legal action; convergence theory asks whether that meaning is stabilized, contested, corrected, or allowed to block legal standing.
